Chemistry of Life

1.1Water & Why It MattersCED 1.1 · SYI-1.A

Water is polar: oxygen hogs the shared electrons, so O is slightly negative (δ−) and each H is slightly positive (δ+). Those partial charges let water molecules grab onto each other with hydrogen bonds — weak individually, mighty in bulk.

The properties — and the exact chemistry behind each

For every property, the cause is the same: H-bonding from polarity
PropertyWhy (mechanism)Biological consequence
CohesionWater H-bonds to waterSurface tension; unbroken water column pulled up xylem in transpiration
AdhesionWater H-bonds to other polar/charged surfacesCapillary action — water climbs narrow tubes against gravity
High specific heatHeat energy first goes to breaking H-bonds, not raising tempOceans/organisms resist temperature swings → stable habitats & body temp
High heat of vaporizationLots of energy needed to break all H-bonds to vaporizeEvaporative cooling — sweating/panting/transpiration removes heat
Less dense as solidH-bonds lock into an open crystal lattice in iceIce floats & insulates water below → aquatic life survives winter
Universal solventPolar water forms hydration shells around ions/polar solutesMedium for nearly all metabolic reactions & transport

Hydrophilic = polar/charged, interacts with water (dissolves). Hydrophobic = nonpolar, excluded by water (oils, fats) — this exclusion is what drives membrane formation (Unit 2).

Acids, bases & pH (concept level)

pH = a logarithmic scale of H⁺ concentration. Acids release H⁺ (lower pH); bases accept H⁺ / release OH⁻ (higher pH). Each step is a 10× change. Buffers resist pH change by absorbing or donating H⁺ — critical for keeping blood/cytoplasm near pH 7.

⚡ On the exam
Every water property traces back to one cause: hydrogen bonding from polarity. Given any scenario (insect striding on a pond, sweat cooling skin, a lake freezing top-down, sap rising in a tree), name the property and explicitly say "because of hydrogen bonding between polar water molecules."
⚠ Classic traps
Hydrogen bonds are intermolecular attractions, not covalent bonds — they form between molecules, while polar covalent bonds are within a water molecule. Don't confuse high specific heat (resists temp change) with high heat of vaporization (powers evaporative cooling).
✎ FRQ tip
Water FRQs want a cause → effect → biological relevance chain. Template: "Because water is polar, it forms hydrogen bonds; this causes [property], which allows [specific organism process]." A property named without the H-bond mechanism loses the point.
— Safe to skip
Exact 104.5° bond angle, specific-heat values in cal/g, and the Henderson–Hasselbalch buffer equation. Know the concepts above, not the math.

1.2Elements of Life & Functional GroupsCED 1.2–1.3 · SYI-1.B

Life is built mostly from CHNOPS: Carbon, Hydrogen, Nitrogen, Oxygen, Phosphorus, Sulfur. Carbon is the universal backbone because it forms four stable covalent bonds, giving endless chains, branches, and rings.

Phosphorus (P)
Only in nucleic acids, phospholipids, and ATP. "See P → think DNA/RNA/ATP/membrane."
Nitrogen (N)
In proteins (amino group) and nucleic acids (bases). Not in carbs or pure fats.
Sulfur (S)
In two amino acids (cysteine/methionine) → forms disulfide bridges that lock protein shape.

Functional groups — recognize them on sight

⚠ Classic trap — element questions are free points
Only nucleic acids & ATP contain phosphorus. Only proteins & nucleic acids contain nitrogen. Carbohydrates & lipids are just C, H, O. If a molecule "contains nitrogen and phosphorus," it's a nucleotide/nucleic acid.

Carbohydrates — α vs β glucose

Same formula, different linkage = totally different molecule. α-glucose chains → starch/glycogen (digestible energy store). β-glucose chains → cellulose (rigid, indigestible to most animals — fiber). This single bond-orientation difference is a favorite exam point.

Proteins — the 4 levels of structure
✓ Quick recall — denaturation
Heat, pH, or salt changes break the weak R-group interactions → protein unfolds (denatures) → loses 3-D shape → loses function (e.g., a fever denaturing enzymes, an egg white cooking). Primary structure (covalent peptide bonds) usually survives; higher levels collapse.
✎ FRQ tip
A single amino-acid substitution FRQ (e.g., sickle-cell): trace DNA mutation → changed amino acid → altered R-group → altered folding/shape → altered/lost function → phenotype. Always connect structure to function explicitly.
— Safe to skip
Memorizing all 20 amino acid names/structures, glycosidic 1→4 vs 1→6 numbering, enantiomer/cis-trans isomer subtypes, and waxes. Know α vs β glucose concept, the 4 protein levels, and the 3 lipid types.

1.4Dehydration Synthesis vs. HydrolysisCED 1.4 · SYI-1.C

Two reactions build and break every macromolecule. They are mirror images — and water is the hinge. Both require enzymes.

⚠ The classic Do-Now question
Two glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) join to form maltose. Why is maltose C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ and not C₁₂H₂₄O₁₂?
Answer: dehydration synthesis removed one H₂O when forming the glycosidic bond. 12+12 C, but 24−2 = 22 H and 12−1 = 11 O. Every bond formed = one water out.
⚡ On the exam
Digestion is hydrolysis (enzymes + water break food polymers into absorbable monomers). Building body tissue/storage is dehydration synthesis. Both require enzymes.

1.6Nucleic Acids & DirectionalityCED 1.6 · IST-1.A/B

A nucleotide = phosphate + 5-carbon sugar + nitrogenous base. Nucleotides link via phosphodiester bonds into a sugar–phosphate backbone.

DNA

  • Double-stranded helix, antiparallel
  • Deoxyribose sugar
  • Bases A, T, G, C
  • A–T (2 H-bonds), G–C (3 H-bonds)
  • Stores genetic information (stable)

RNA

  • Single-stranded
  • Ribose sugar
  • Bases A, U, G, C (uracil replaces thymine)
  • mRNA, tRNA, rRNA
  • Expresses information (transient)
⚡ On the exam
Chargaff's rule: %A = %T and %G = %C. Given one strand, write the complement (A↔T, G↔C; A↔U for RNA). Always know 5′→3′ directionality — it's why replication has a leading vs lagging strand (Unit 6).
⚠ Trap
Purines (Adenine, Guanine) are the double-ring bases — "Pure As Gold." Pyrimidines (Cytosine, Thymine, Uracil) are single-ring. A purine always pairs with a pyrimidine (keeps the helix a constant width).
UNIT 2

Cell Structure & Function

10–13% of exam
In this unit 2.1 Prokaryote vs eukaryote 2.1–2.2 Organelles & endomembrane 2.11–2.12 Endosymbiotic theory 2.3 Surface area : volume 2.4–2.5 Membrane & permeability 2.6–2.7, 2.9 Transport 2.8 Tonicity & water potential

2.1Cell Theory & Two Kinds of CellsCED 2.1 · SYI-1.E

Cell theory: (1) all living things are made of cells, (2) the cell is the basic unit of life, (3) all cells come from pre-existing cells.

Prokaryote

  • No nucleus — DNA loose in nucleoid
  • No membrane-bound organelles
  • Small, simple, bacteria/archaea
  • Has: ribosomes, cell wall, plasma membrane, cytoplasm
  • Better at: reproducing fast, surviving extremes

Eukaryote

  • True nucleus (membrane-bound DNA)
  • Membrane-bound organelles
  • Larger, complex, plant/animal/fungi/protist
  • Compartments = specialized reactions
  • Better at: specialization, large multicellular bodies
✓ Quick recall — structure ↔ function (heavily tested)
  • RBC has no nucleus (in mammals) → maximizes O₂-carrying volume; biconcave for surface area. Trap: frog RBCs DO have a nucleus.
  • Neuron — long axon + dendrites to transmit signals far & fast.
  • Muscle cell — packed with mitochondria & striated proteins for ATP-fueled contraction.
  • Root hair cell — long projection for water/mineral absorption; no chloroplasts (underground).

Shared by ALL cells (prokaryote & eukaryote): plasma membrane, cytoplasm/cytosol, ribosomes, and DNA. Prokaryotes have 70S ribosomes & a circular chromosome in a nucleoid (no membrane); eukaryotes have 80S ribosomes & linear DNA in a true nucleus.

⚡ On the exam
Compartmentalization is the eukaryote superpower: membrane-bound organelles let incompatible reactions run simultaneously in separate optimized environments (e.g., digestive enzymes safely sealed in a lysosome). Prokaryotes win on speed & reproduction rate.

2.2Organelles & the Endomembrane SystemCED 2.1–2.2, 2.10 · SYI-1.E/F

An organelle is to a cell what an organ is to a body: a structure with a specialized job. Plant cells add three things animals lack: cell wall, chloroplasts, large central vacuole. Animal cells have centrioles & lysosomes; plants generally don't.

⚠ Classic traps
  • Cytoskeleton = microtubules + microfilaments + intermediate filaments. The one that anchors the nucleus in place is intermediate filaments.
  • Cytosol = the fluid; cytoplasm = fluid + everything in it; cytoskeleton = the protein scaffold. Don't mix these up.
  • Bound ribosomes make proteins destined to be secreted or membrane-embedded; free ribosomes make cytoplasmic proteins.

2.3Endosymbiotic TheoryCED 2.11–2.12 · IST-1.J

Mitochondria & chloroplasts were once free-living prokaryotes engulfed by a larger ancestral cell. They were never digested — they stayed and became permanent partners. This also links to evolution (Unit 7).

⚡ On the exam — the 4 evidences (memorize)
  1. Their own circular DNA (like bacteria)
  2. Their own 70S ribosomes (prokaryote-type, not the cell's 80S)
  3. Double membrane (outer = host vesicle, inner = original bacterium)
  4. Reproduce by binary fission, independent of the cell, and are bacterium-sized

2.4Surface Area : Volume RatioCED 2.3 · ENE-1.A · formula sheet

As a cell grows, volume grows faster than surface area. The membrane (surface) handles all exchange; the cytoplasm (volume) makes all demand. Too big → can't import/export fast enough → dies. This limits cell size.

★ AP formula sheet
Cube: SA = 6s², V = s³. Sphere: SA = 4πr², V = 4/3 πr³. Cylinder volume = πr²h. The exam gives these — you just plug in & compare ratios. Higher SA:V = faster exchange.
✎ FRQ tip
"Which cell exchanges materials more efficiently?" → calculate SA:V for each; the higher ratio wins. Connect to why mitochondria have cristae and intestinal cells have microvilli (folding maximizes surface area).

2.5Membrane Structure & PermeabilityCED 2.4–2.5 · ENE-2.A/B

The fluid mosaic model: a phospholipid bilayer studded with proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrate tags — fluid because phospholipids drift sideways, mosaic because of the varied embedded proteins.

⚡ Selective permeability — what crosses freely
The hydrophobic tail core is the gatekeeper. Crosses easily: small nonpolar (O₂, CO₂), lipid-soluble (steroids), tiny uncharged. Needs a protein: ions, glucose, amino acids, large polar molecules. Water crosses slowly alone, fast through aquaporins. Membrane protein jobs: transport, enzymatic activity, signal reception, cell-cell recognition, attachment/anchoring.

What controls membrane fluidity

Temperature
Higher temp → more fluid; cold → phospholipids pack & the membrane can solidify.
Unsaturated fatty acid tails
Kinks prevent tight packing → more fluid. More saturated tails → more rigid.
Cholesterol = "fluidity buffer"
At high temp it restrains movement (less fluid); at low temp it spaces phospholipids apart so they can't solidify (more fluid). It resists change in either direction.
⚠ Trap
Cholesterol doesn't simply "increase" or "decrease" fluidity — it buffers it (temperature-dependent). Cold-adapted organisms put more unsaturated tails in their membranes to stay fluid.

Molecules move by random motion down a concentration gradient; we track net movement (both directions occur — net is high→low) until equilibrium.

Facilitated diffusion uses a protein but is still PASSIVE (no ATP — it goes down the gradient). "Uses a protein" ≠ "active." Active transport is defined by going against the gradient using ATP.

2.8Tonicity & Water PotentialCED 2.8 · ENE-2.E · formula sheet

✓ Quick recall — say it out loud
"Hyper = more solute outside → water OUT → cell shrinks. Hypo = less solute outside → water IN → cell swells/lyses. Iso = equal → no net change." Water moves toward solute.
⚡ On the exam
Marine organisms live in a hypertonic ocean → constantly lose water (must drink & pump out salt). Freshwater organisms live in a hypotonic environment → constantly gain water (must pump it out, e.g. contractile vacuole). Osmoregulation is a "characteristic of life" theme.
⚠ Plant vs. animal in hypotonic
Hypotonic is dangerous for animal cells (lyse) but ideal for plant cells — the rigid cell wall lets them become firm/turgid instead of bursting. A wilted plant is in a hypertonic/dry situation (plasmolysis).

Water potential (Ψ) — the AP calculation

Water potential predicts which way water moves: water always flows from HIGH Ψ to LOW (more negative) Ψ. Pure water in an open container = Ψ = 0.

★ AP formula sheet
Ψ = Ψp + Ψs Ψp = pressure potential · Ψs = solute potential Ψs = − i C R T i = ionization constant (sucrose = 1, NaCl = 2) · C = molarity · R = 0.0831 L·bar/(mol·K) · T = Kelvin (°C + 273)
▶ Worked — which way does water move?
Setup: a 0.15 M sucrose solution at 25 °C, open beaker (so Ψp = 0).
Ψs = −(1)(0.15)(0.0831)(298) = −3.71 bars
Ψ = Ψp + Ψs = 0 + (−3.71) = −3.71 bars
If a potato cell inside has Ψ = −2.0 bars, water moves from the cell (−2.0, higher) → solution (−3.71, lower). The cell loses water & shrinks.
⚠ The #1 water-potential trap
Adding solute makes Ψs more negative → lowers Ψ. Water moves toward lower (more negative) Ψ = toward more solute. "Hypotonic/hypertonic" describe the solution, not the cell.
✎ FRQ tip
Classic dialysis-tubing / potato-core lab FRQ: compute Ψ for solution and cell, state direction of net water movement, predict mass change, and identify controls/variables. Always show the sign.

3.1Enzymes & InhibitionCED 3.1–3.2 · ENE-1

Enzymes are biological catalysts (almost always proteins) that lower activation energy (Eₐ) by stabilizing the transition state. They are not consumed, are reusable, are specific to a substrate, and do not change ΔG or the equilibrium — only the rate.

Specificity & induced fit
The substrate binds the active site; the enzyme changes shape slightly to grip it (induced fit, not a rigid lock-and-key). One enzyme, one reaction.
Factors affecting rate
Temperature & pH: rate rises to an optimum then crashes (denaturation breaks H-bonds → active site deforms). [Substrate]: rate rises then plateaus at saturation. [Enzyme]: more enzyme = higher rate (if substrate available).

Three kinds of inhibition

✓ Feedback inhibition
The final product of a pathway inhibits an early enzyme in that same pathway → the cell stops making what it already has enough of. This is negative feedback at the molecular level (links to Unit 4).
⚡ On the exam — read the graph
Rate vs temp/pH: peaks at the optimum then sharply drops (denaturation) — it does NOT keep rising. Rate vs [substrate]: rises then plateaus (enzymes saturated). Competitive inhibition: same max rate but needs more substrate to get there; noncompetitive: lower max rate.
⚠ Trap
Enzymes lower Eₐ but do not change ΔG, the equilibrium, or whether a reaction is spontaneous — only how fast equilibrium is reached. They are not "used up."

3.2Free Energy & ATPCED 3.3 · ENE-1 · formula sheet

Exergonic (−ΔG)
Releases free energy, spontaneous (e.g. cellular respiration, ATP hydrolysis).
Endergonic (+ΔG)
Requires energy input, non-spontaneous (e.g. building macromolecules, active transport).
Energy coupling
The cell pays for endergonic reactions by coupling them to exergonic ATP hydrolysis.
★ AP formula sheet
ΔG = ΔH − TΔS ΔG < 0 → exergonic/spontaneous · ΔG > 0 → endergonic · ΔH = enthalpy, ΔS = entropy, T = Kelvin. Maintaining order (low entropy) requires constant free-energy input.

3.4Photosynthesis deep — high yieldCED 3.4 · ENE-1

Plants convert light energy into chemical energy (sugar). Happens in the chloroplast, in two linked stages.

6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂

Stage 1 — Light Reactions (in the thylakoid membrane)

  1. Photosystem II absorbs light → energizes electrons. Water is split (photolysis): replaces lost e⁻, releases O₂ as byproduct, and dumps H⁺ into the lumen.
  2. Electrons fall down the electron transport chain; the cytochrome complex pumps H⁺ into the lumen → builds a gradient.
  3. Photosystem I re-energizes electrons → used to make NADPH.
  4. H⁺ flows back out through ATP synthase → chemiosmosis makes ATP (ADP + Pi → ATP).

Stage 2 — Calvin Cycle (in the stroma)

Uses the ATP + NADPH to fix CO₂ into sugar. Three phases — track it as a wheel that turns 3× to net one G3P:

⚡ On the exam — must know cold
  • O₂ comes from splitting WATER, not CO₂. (Super common MCQ.)
  • Light reactions: input light + H₂O → output ATP + NADPH + O₂. Calvin: input CO₂ + ATP + NADPH → output G3P (sugar).
  • Calvin cycle is "light-independent" but stops in the dark because it runs out of ATP/NADPH.
  • RuBisCO = the CO₂-fixing enzyme (most abundant protein on Earth).
✓ Pigments
Chlorophyll a = primary pigment. Chlorophyll b & carotenoids = accessory pigments (broaden the light captured, pass energy to chlorophyll a). Chlorophyll absorbs red & blue, reflects green → that's why leaves look green.
✎ FRQ tip — experimental design
Common labs: floating leaf-disk assay or DPIP (measures light reactions). Identify independent variable (light intensity, CO₂, wavelength), dependent variable (O₂ produced / disks floating / rate), constants, and a control. Predict & justify the curve. "More light → faster rate until another factor (CO₂, enzymes) becomes limiting."
— Safe to skip
C4 / CAM / photorespiration mechanisms, exact absorption-spectrum nm values, cyclic photophosphorylation detail, and every electron carrier name (plastoquinone, plastocyanin, ferredoxin). Know the inputs/outputs, locations, and that O₂ comes from water.

3.5Cellular RespirationCED 3.5 · ENE-1

The reverse logic: break glucose to harvest ATP. Aerobic (with O₂) yields ~30–38 ATP per glucose (the exact number is approximate — don't memorize one value).

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂ → 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + ~30–38 ATP
The 4 stages — location is heavily tested
StageWhereIn → OutATP
GlycolysisCytoplasmGlucose → 2 pyruvate; makes 2 NADHnet 2 (substrate-level)
Pyruvate oxidationMito. matrixPyruvate → acetyl-CoA; releases CO₂; makes NADH0

✓ Quick recall — the mirror
Photosynthesis and respiration are opposites: photosynthesis stores energy & builds glucose (in chloroplast); respiration releases energy & breaks glucose (in mitochondria). Both use an electron transport chain + chemiosmosis (ATP synthase) — the universal ATP trick.
⚡ On the exam — must know cold
O₂ is the FINAL electron acceptor in the ETC (it pulls electrons through & forms H₂O). O₂ is only used at the very end — NOT in glycolysis or Krebs. Most ATP comes from chemiosmosis (H⁺ flowing through ATP synthase down its gradient).

3.5bFermentation & Anaerobic RespirationCED 3.5 · ENE-1

No O₂ → no ETC → only glycolysis (2 ATP) runs. Fermentation's job is not to make ATP — it's to regenerate NAD⁺ so glycolysis can keep going.

✓ Photosynthesis ↔ respiration: the mirror
They are reverse processes. Both use an electron transport chain + chemiosmosis through ATP synthase. Both occur in double-membrane, endosymbiotic-origin organelles. Photosynthesis stores energy (anabolic, chloroplast); respiration releases it (catabolic, mitochondria).
⚠ Top traps
Fermentation itself makes 0 ATP (the 2 ATP are from glycolysis). Its purpose = recycle NAD⁺. "Anaerobic" = no O₂. Aerobic respiration's huge yield depends entirely on O₂ being present at the ETC.
— Safe to skip
Every Krebs intermediate (citrate, isocitrate, α-ketoglutarate…), exact ATP-per-NADH ratios (2.5/1.5), naming ETC complexes I–IV, and detailed glycolysis steps. Know stages, locations, inputs/outputs, and the role of O₂ & chemiosmosis.

4.1Cell Communication & Signal TransductionCED 4.1–4.4 · IST-3

Cells coordinate by sending & receiving chemical signals. Distance matters:

Direct contact
Gap junctions (animal) / plasmodesmata (plant); surface-marker recognition.
Local signaling
Paracrine (to nearby cells) & synaptic (neurotransmitters across a synapse).
Long-distance
Endocrine — hormones travel through the bloodstream to distant targets.

The 3 universal stages

⚡ On the exam
Name all three stages in order. Hydrophobic signals (steroids) use intracellular receptors; hydrophilic signals use membrane receptors. A mutation in a receptor or relay protein can block the response or leave it permanently "on" (→ disease/cancer) — a classic FRQ prompt.
✎ FRQ tip
"Predict the effect of a mutation in [pathway component]." Walk the cascade: if reception fails → no response even with signal; if a relay is stuck active → response without a signal. Always tie back to the cell's final response.

4.2FeedbackCED 4.5 · ENE-3

Negative feedback

  • Output reverses/reduces the process
  • Restores set point → homeostasis
  • Blood glucose (insulin/glucagon), body temp, ATP inhibiting its own pathway

Positive feedback

  • Output amplifies the process
  • Drives a change to completion
  • Childbirth (oxytocin), blood clotting, fruit ripening, action potential
⚠ Trap
Negative feedback is the common homeostasis case (most body systems). Positive feedback is rarer & pushes away from equilibrium toward an endpoint — it is NOT "bad," just amplifying. Don't default every answer to negative feedback.

4.3The Cell Cycle & MitosisCED 4.6 · IST-1

Interphase (G1 → S → G2) is most of a cell's life: it grows, then copies its DNA in S phase, then preps to divide. Mitosis + cytokinesis splits one cell into two genetically identical daughters.

✓ Quick recall
Animal cytokinesis = cleavage furrow pinches in. Plant cytokinesis = cell plate forms a new wall (no pinching — rigid wall). Mitosis result: 2 cells, diploid, genetically identical to parent. Used for growth, repair, asexual reproduction.

4.4Regulation, Checkpoints & CancerCED 4.7 · IST-1

The cycle is policed at checkpoints (G1, G2, M) that ask: is the DNA okay? is the cell big enough? are chromosomes attached? If not, the cycle halts for repair.

  • Proto-oncogenes → "go" signals. Mutate into oncogenes = gas pedal stuck down.
  • Tumor-suppressor genes (e.g. p53) → "stop/repair" signals. Mutate = brakes cut.
  • Cancer = unregulated cell division → ignores checkpoints & apoptosis → forms a tumor; metastasis = spread.
⚡ On the exam
Cancer needs multiple mutations in genes controlling the cycle (it's not one switch). p53 is the famous tumor suppressor / "guardian of the genome." Cell signaling (ligand → receptor → signal transduction → response) is how cells coordinate division — a growth factor binding a receptor is the trigger.
✓ Apoptosis
Apoptosis = programmed, controlled cell death that removes damaged/dangerous cells (e.g. cells that fail a checkpoint, or webbing between fetal fingers). It's protective — cancer cells often evade apoptosis.
— Safe to skip
Specific cyclin/CDK pair names & concentration curves, MPF, caspase cascades, and detailed second-messenger chemistry. Know the concept: checkpoints + regulatory proteins control division; losing that control (oncogenes + broken tumor suppressors + evaded apoptosis) = cancer.

5.1Meiosis & Genetic VariationCED 5.1–5.2 · IST-1

Meiosis makes gametes (sex cells): one diploid (2n) cell → four haploid (n) cells that are genetically different. Two divisions, one DNA replication.

⚡ On the exam — phase ID (heavily tested)
  • DNA replication → Interphase (S phase), before meiosis even starts.
  • Crossing over & synapsis (homologs pair as a tetrad, swap segments at the chiasma) → Prophase I.
  • Independent assortment (homolog pairs line up & orient randomly) → Metaphase I.
  • Crossing over + independent assortment + random fertilization = genetic variation.
✓ Chromosome math
If diploid 2n = 28 → each gamete has 14; a zygote from two such gametes = back to 28. Mitosis keeps the number; meiosis halves it; fertilization restores it.

Mitosis

  • 1 division → 2 cells
  • Diploid, identical to parent
  • Growth, repair, asexual
  • No crossing over / no tetrads

Meiosis

  • 2 divisions → 4 cells
  • Haploid, all different
  • Makes gametes (sexual repro)
  • Crossing over + independent assortment
⚡ Three sources of genetic variation
  1. Crossing over (prophase I) — homologs swap segments at the chiasma
  2. Independent assortment (metaphase I) — random orientation of homolog pairs → 2ⁿ combinations (humans: 2²³ ≈ 8 million per parent)
  3. Random fertilization — any sperm × any egg
Together these supply the raw variation natural selection acts on (links to Unit 7).

5.3Mendelian Genetics & ProbabilityCED 5.3 · EVO-2 / IST-1

  • Law of Segregation: the two alleles for a gene separate into different gametes (one per gamete).
  • Law of Independent Assortment: genes on different chromosomes are inherited independently.
  • Dominant masks recessive; genotype (alleles, e.g. Bb) vs phenotype (trait shown); homozygous vs heterozygous. A test cross (× homozygous recessive) reveals an unknown genotype.
✓ Probability rules — faster than big Punnett squares
Product rule (AND): chance of two independent events both happening = multiply. P(aabb) from AaBb×AaBb = ¼ × ¼ = 1/16.
Sum rule (OR): chance of either mutually exclusive event = add. For a dihybrid, "1 dominant + 1 recessive trait" = combine the relevant products. Use these instead of drawing a 16-box grid.

⚠ Top traps

5.4bChi-Square — The Full Workflow do exactly thisformula sheet

Chi-square asks: "Is the difference between what I observed and what I expected just chance, or is something real going on?"

χ² = Σ (observed − expected)² / expected df = (# categories − 1)
▶ Worked example — dihybrid F₂, 9:3:3:1

A cross predicts a 9:3:3:1 ratio. You count 556 offspring: 315, 108, 101, 32.

Step 1 — State H₀ (null): "The offspring follow the expected 9:3:3:1 Mendelian ratio (the genes assort independently). Any difference is due to chance."
Step 2 — Expected = total × ratio fraction. 556 × 9/16 = 312.75; ×3/16 = 104.25; ×3/16 = 104.25; ×1/16 = 34.75.
Step 3 — Compute each (O−E)²/E:
(315−312.75)²/312.75 = 0.016 (108−104.25)²/104.25 = 0.135 (101−104.25)²/104.25 = 0.101 ( 32− 34.75)²/ 34.75 = 0.218 χ² = 0.016+0.135+0.101+0.218 = 0.47
Step 4 — df = 4 categories − 1 = 3. Critical value at p = 0.05, df = 3 → 7.81.
Step 5 — Decide & write the conclusion in these words:
Since χ² (0.47) < critical value (7.81), we fail to reject the null hypothesis. There is not statistical evidence that the offspring deviate from the expected Mendelian ratio — the difference is due to chance.
⚠ The decision rule — never get this backwards
  • χ² < critical value → FAIL TO REJECT H₀. Data fits the expected ratio. "No statistical evidence that it deviates."
  • χ² > critical value → REJECT H₀. Data does not fit. Something real is going on (e.g. genes are linked, not independently assorting).
  • p = 0.05 is the cutoff. df = (categories − 1), not sample size − 1.

5.4cLinkage & Recombination FrequencyCED 5.3 · IST-1

Linked genes sit close together on the same chromosome → inherited together → fewer recombinants than expected. Crossing over occasionally separates them; the more often = the farther apart.

RF = (# recombinant offspring) / (total offspring) × 100% RF% ≈ map distance in centimorgans (cM)
▶ Worked example — recombination frequency

A test cross gives four phenotypes. The two large classes are parental; the two small classes are recombinants:

parental: 420 + 400 = 820 recombinant: 90 + 90 = 180 total = 1000 RF = 180 / 1000 = 18% → genes ≈ 18 cM apart

Smaller RF (e.g. 90/1000 = 9% → 9 cM) = genes are closer together & more tightly linked. RF maxes out near 50% (then they behave as unlinked / independently assorting).

⚠ Classic traps
  • Recombinants are the SMALL groups, parentals are the large groups. Don't add the wrong pair.
  • RF can never exceed 50%. 50% = effectively unlinked.
  • This connects to chi-square: if a 1:1:1:1 testcross fails the chi-square (you reject H₀), that's your evidence the genes are linked — then report RF as the map distance.

5.5Nondisjunction & Chromosomal DisordersCED 5.5 · IST-1

Nondisjunction = chromosomes fail to separate during meiosis → gametes with the wrong number → aneuploidy after fertilization.

⚡ On the exam
Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) = 3 copies of chromosome 21. Sex-chromosome aneuploidy: XXY (Klinefelter), XO (Turner). Polyploidy (full extra sets) is common & often viable in plants — a route to instant sympatric speciation (Unit 7).

Gene Expression & Regulation

6.1DNA & RNA StructureCED 6.1 · IST-1

DNA is a double helix of nucleotides (phosphate + deoxyribose sugar + nitrogen base). The two strands are antiparallel and held by complementary base pairs. (Full structure detail & directionality is in Unit 1.6.)

⚡ On the exam
Chargaff: %A = %T and %G = %C. If a strand is given, write the complement (A↔T, G↔C). DNA → RNA → protein is the central dogma.

6.2DNA ReplicationCED 6.2 · IST-1

Replication is semiconservative: each new DNA molecule = one original strand + one new strand (proved by Meselson–Stahl). High fidelity ensures genetic information passes accurately to daughter cells.

✓ AP-level depth
You need: semiconservative, helicase unwinds, DNA polymerase synthesizes 5′→3′, leading vs lagging strand concept, and that base-pairing guarantees an accurate copy. That's it.
— Safe to skip
Naming primase, RNA primers, DNA ligase, topoisomerase, single-strand binding proteins, Okazaki-fragment mechanics. The CED only expects helicase, DNA polymerase, and the leading/lagging concept.

6.3Transcription & RNA ProcessingCED 6.3 · IST-1

In the nucleus, RNA polymerase binds the promoter, reads the DNA template strand (3′→5′), and builds a complementary mRNA (5′→3′). RNA differences: single strand, ribose sugar, and uracil (U) replaces thymine (T).

▶ Worked — DNA template → mRNA
DNA template (3'→5'): T A C G G A T T T A C T mRNA (5'→3'): A U G C C U A A A U G A (start) (stop)
Rule: A→U, T→A, C→G, G→C. T becomes U. AUG = start codon; UAA/UAG/UGA = stop.

Eukaryotic RNA processing (before mRNA leaves the nucleus)

⚠ Trap
EXons are EXpressed; INtrons are IN-the-trash (removed). Processing is eukaryote-only — prokaryotes lack a nucleus, so they transcribe & translate simultaneously.

6.4Translation (mRNA → protein)CED 6.4 · IST-1

At the ribosome (rRNA + protein), mRNA is read in 3-base codons. Each codon is matched by a tRNA carrying an anticodon and its specific amino acid. Amino acids link via peptide bonds → polypeptide → protein. Starts at AUG (Met), ends at a stop codon (UAA/UAG/UGA) → release.

✓ Properties of the genetic code
Redundant/degenerate (multiple codons → same amino acid) but unambiguous (one codon → only one amino acid). Nearly universal across all life → strong evidence of common ancestry (Unit 7). Read in non-overlapping triplets.
✎ FRQ tip
Given a DNA strand: transcribe → translate using the codon chart (provided). Then predict how a stated mutation changes the protein & phenotype. Always go DNA → mRNA → amino acids → protein function → trait.

6.5Gene Regulation & Cell SpecializationCED 6.5–6.6 · IST-2

Every cell has the same DNA but expresses different genes — differential gene expression is why a neuron ≠ a muscle cell. This is THE answer to "how do cells with identical DNA differ?"

Prokaryotic operons

lac operon — inducible

  • Catabolic (digests lactose)
  • Default OFF; lactose present → repressor released → genes ON
  • "Turn on only when the food is there"

trp operon — repressible

  • Anabolic (builds tryptophan)
  • Default ON; tryptophan present → acts as corepressor → genes OFF
  • "Stop making it when you have enough"
✓ Eukaryotic regulation & epigenetics
Eukaryotes use transcription factors + enhancers/promoters. Epigenetics = heritable expression changes without changing DNA sequence: DNA methylation silences genes; histone acetylation loosens chromatin → activates. Environment can trigger these.
⚠ Trap
lac = inducible (turned on by its substrate) · trp = repressible (turned off by its product). Mixing these up is the single most common operon error.

6.6MutationsCED 6.7 · IST-2

⚡ On the exam
Mutations are the ultimate source of all genetic variation (raw material for evolution). Only mutations in gametes are heritable. Chromosomal: duplication, deletion, inversion, translocation. Prokaryotes also gain variation by horizontal gene transfer (transformation, transduction, conjugation).

6.7BiotechnologyCED 6.8 · IST-1

Gel electrophoresis
Separates DNA by size. DNA is negative → moves toward the + electrode. Smaller fragments travel farther.
PCR
Amplifies DNA: cycles of denature → anneal (primers) → extend (Taq polymerase). Makes millions of copies.
Bacterial transformation
Insert a gene into a plasmid (cut with restriction enzymes), put into bacteria; antibiotic-resistance marker selects successful cells. Makes recombinant products (e.g. insulin).
DNA sequencing / CRISPR
Sequencing = read the base order. CRISPR = targeted gene editing (concept level).
— Safe to skip
Thermocycler temperatures, primer Tm/GC math, specific restriction-site sequences, Sanger vs next-gen chemistry. Know each tool's purpose and how to read a gel.
UNIT 7

Natural Selection

13–20% — highest weight
In this unit 7.1–7.4 Selection & fitness 7.6–7.7 Evidence & common ancestry 7.5 Hardy–Weinberg 7.8, 7.10 Drift, flow, speciation 7.9 Phylogeny 7.12 Origin of life

7.1Natural Selection & FitnessCED 7.1–7.4 · EVO-1

Darwin's logic: organisms overproduce → there is variation → some variants survive & reproduce better (differential fitness) → those traits become more common over generations.

  • Fitness = relative reproductive success (not strength/size). The trait must be heritable to be selected.
  • Selection acts on phenotypes; evolution = change in allele frequencies in a population over time. Individuals don't evolve — populations do.
  • Selection pressures come from the environment; in stable environments selection slows, in changing ones it accelerates.
⚠ The misconception graveyard (huge on this exam)
  • Organisms do NOT "try to adapt" or mutate because they need to (that's Lamarck — wrong). Mutations are random; selection is not.
  • "Survival of the fittest" means reproduction, not just survival.
  • Natural selection acts on the phenotype, only indirectly on genotype.
  • Individuals can't evolve in their lifetime — allele frequencies shift across generations.

Types of selection

✓ Sexual & artificial selection
Sexual selection: traits that boost mating success (bright plumage, antlers) spread even if they hurt survival → sexual dimorphism. Artificial selection: humans act as the selective agent (dog breeds, crops) — proves selection can rapidly reshape populations.

7.2Evidence & Common AncestryCED 7.6–7.7 · EVO-2

⚡ The full evidence list
Fossils (transitional forms, dating) · comparative anatomy (homologous/vestigial) · embryology (similar development) · biogeography (island/marsupial distributions) · molecular (DNA/protein similarity, e.g. cytochrome c) · direct observation (antibiotic/pesticide resistance, peppered moth, Galápagos finches).
✓ Common ancestry of ALL life
Shared core features prove one origin: DNA/RNA as genetic material, the near-universal genetic code, ribosomes, ATP, and common metabolic pathways (glycolysis). The more conserved a molecule, the more fundamental its role.

7.3Hardy–Weinberg plug & chugCED 7.5 · EVO-1 · formula sheet

A null model: if a population is not evolving, allele frequencies stay constant. Deviation = evolution is happening.

p + q = 1 p = freq(dominant allele), q = freq(recessive allele) p² + 2pq + q² = 1 p² = homo-dom, 2pq = hetero, q² = homo-recessive
▶ Worked — find the carriers

9% of a population shows the recessive disease (aa). Find allele & genotype frequencies.

q² = 0.09 → q = √0.09 = 0.3
p = 1 − q = 1 − 0.3 = 0.7
Carriers 2pq = 2(0.7)(0.3) = 0.42 → 42% are heterozygous carriers
p² = 0.49 → 49% homozygous dominant. Check: 0.49 + 0.42 + 0.09 = 1 ✓
⚡ On the exam
Always start from q² (the recessive phenotype) because it's the only genotype you can see directly. 5 conditions for equilibrium (= NOT evolving): no mutation, no selection, no gene flow (migration), random mating, very large population. Break any one → allele frequencies change → evolution.

7.4Drift, Gene Flow & SpeciationCED 7.8, 7.10 · EVO / SYI-3

What changes allele frequencies (microevolution)

Mutation
The ultimate source of new alleles (rare but original).
Gene flow
Migration moving alleles between populations → makes populations more similar.
Genetic drift
Random change, strong in small populations. Bottleneck (crash, e.g. cheetahs) & founder effect (few colonize a new area). Not adaptive.
Nonrandom mating
Inbreeding/assortative mating — shifts genotype frequencies.
Natural selection
The only non-random, adaptive mechanism.

Speciation

⚡ On the exam
Biological species = can interbreed & produce viable, fertile offspring. Prezygotic barriers stop mating/fertilization (habitat, temporal, behavioral, mechanical, gametic); postzygotic stop viable/fertile hybrids (mule = sterile). Pace: gradualism vs punctuated equilibrium (rapid bursts + long stasis).
✎ FRQ tip
"Is this drift or selection?" — Random + small population + no fitness difference = drift. Directional change tied to a survival/reproductive advantage = selection. Low genetic diversity (from drift/bottleneck) → population is vulnerable to extinction.

7.5Phylogeny & ClassificationCED 7.9 · SYI-1

  • Taxonomy: Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species ("Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Soup"). Three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya.
  • Speciation = formation of new species when gene flow stops & populations diverge. Allopatric = geographic barrier; sympatric = same area (e.g. polyploidy in plants). Species = can interbreed & produce fertile offspring.
  • Homologous structures = same origin, divergent (common ancestor). Analogous = different origin, same function (convergent evolution — don't use for the tree).
⚠ Classic trap
Reading trees: relatedness = most recent common ancestor (shared node), NOT how close the tip labels are written or "how many branches." Analogous structures (bird vs. insect wing) show convergent evolution and must not be used to infer ancestry.
— Safe to skip
Cladogram parsimony calculation steps, molecular-clock math, every reproductive-isolation subtype name, and full kingdom characteristics tables. Know homologous vs analogous, allopatric vs sympatric, and how to read a tree.

7.6Origin of Life on EarthCED 7.12 · SYI-3

✓ Why RNA first?
RNA can both store genetic information AND act as an enzyme (ribozyme) — so it could self-replicate before DNA & proteins existed. Endosymbiosis (Unit 2) is key evidence for how complex eukaryotic cells arose.

8.1Responses to the EnvironmentCED 8.1 · ENE-3 / IST-5

Innate behavior
Genetically programmed, present without learning (reflexes, fixed action patterns, taxis/kinesis). Same in all individuals.
Learned behavior
Modified by experience (habituation, imprinting, conditioning, trial-and-error).
Communication & cooperation
Signals (chemical, visual, auditory) coordinate mating, warning, foraging. Cooperative behavior (e.g. social insects) can raise group survival.
⚡ On the exam

8.3Population EcologyCED 8.3–8.4 · SYI-1 · formula sheet

★ AP formula sheet
Population growth: dN/dt = B − D Exponential: dN/dt = rmax N (J-curve) Logistic: dN/dt = rmax N [(K − N) / K] (S-curve) N = population size · K = carrying capacity · r = per-capita growth rate. As N → K, the bracket → 0, so growth slows to zero.
Density-dependent limits
Stronger when crowded: competition, predation, disease, food/space. Regulate near K.
Density-independent limits
Hit regardless of size: weather, fire, natural disasters, human disturbance.
r vs K strategists
r: many offspring, no care, Type III survivorship, unstable habitats (insects, weeds). K: few offspring, parental care, Type I, stable habitats (elephants, humans).
✎ FRQ tip
Given B, D, N, K — plug into the logistic equation; if N < K growth is positive & slowing, at N = K growth = 0. Age-structure pyramids: wide base = rapid future growth. Always interpret the graph, don't just define terms.

★ AP formula sheet — Simpson's Diversity Index
Diversity = 1 − Σ (n/N)² n = # individuals of one species · N = total individuals. Result near 1 = high diversity, near 0 = low. Just plug in the table of counts.

8.2Energy Flow & Trophic LevelsCED 8.2 · ENE-1

Energy enters as sunlight, is captured by producers, and flows one way up the chain — only ~10% transfers between trophic levels (the rest is lost as heat / used in metabolism).

⚡ On the exam
Energy flow is one-way and lost (~90%) at each level; it is not recycled. Matter (C, N, H₂O) IS recycled via biogeochemical cycles. Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) return nutrients to the soil — without them, cycles stop. Producers = base of every pyramid.

8.2bBiogeochemical CyclesCED 8.2 · ENE-1

✓ Phosphorus & water cycles
Phosphorus: NO atmospheric/gas phase — reservoir is rock/sediment; released by weathering; often the limiting nutrient in aquatic systems. Water: evaporation/transpiration ↑ → condensation → precipitation ↓ → runoff/groundwater. Big idea: matter cycles, energy flows (one-way).
— Safe to skip
Every named bacterium genus, exact nutrient-flux numbers, and detailed reservoir tonnages. Know each cycle's main reservoir, how organisms move it, and that decomposers + bacteria are essential.

8.5Disruptions to EcosystemsCED 8.7 · SYI-3 / IST-4

Invasive species
No natural predators → outcompete natives → biodiversity drops.
Human impact
Climate change (fossil-fuel CO₂ → greenhouse warming), eutrophication (fertilizer runoff → algal bloom → O₂ crash → dead zone), habitat loss, deforestation.
Keystone/trophic disruption
Removing a keystone or top predator → trophic cascade reshapes the whole community.
Resilience
Higher biodiversity → ecosystem better resists & recovers from disturbance.
⚡ On the exam
Be ready to predict the ripple effect: "remove species X / introduce invasive Y / warm the climate → trace consequences through the food web and nutrient cycles." Connect disruptions back to energy flow, cycles, and population growth.
✎ FRQ tip
Ecology & evolution dominate the FRQ section. Practice: graph interpretation (growth, selection, energy), Hardy–Weinberg & chi-square calculations, and experimental design (state hypothesis, IV, DV, control, predicted result + justification).
