UNIT 1

Chemistry of Life

Water · Biomolecules

1.1Water & Why It Matters

Water is polar: oxygen hogs the shared electrons, so O is slightly negative (δ−) and each H is slightly positive (δ+). Those partial charges let water molecules grab onto each other with hydrogen bonds — weak individually, mighty in bulk.

The five properties (these get tested)

  • Cohesion — water sticks to water (H-bonds). Drives surface tension and water transport up plant xylem.
  • Adhesion — water sticks to other polar surfaces; helps capillary action against gravity.
  • High specific heat — H-bonds absorb a lot of heat before temperature rises → stable environments & body temp.
  • Evaporative cooling — the highest-energy molecules leave as vapor, cooling what remains (sweating).
  • Ice floats / less dense solid — H-bonds lock into an open lattice; ice insulates ponds so life survives below.
  • Universal solvent — dissolves polar & ionic solutes (hydration shells). Nonpolar = hydrophobic, won't dissolve.
⚡ On the exam
Every water property traces back to one cause: hydrogen bonding from polarity. If a question gives you a scenario (bug walking on water, sweat cooling skin, lake freezing top-down), name the property and say "because of hydrogen bonds."
— Safe to skip
Exact bond angle (104.5°), the math of specific heat in cal/g, and pH buffer equilibrium equations. Know acids release H⁺ (low pH), bases accept H⁺ (high pH), and the scale is logarithmic — not the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation.

1.2The Four Macromolecules

All four are polymers built from monomers — except lipids, which are not true polymers (no repeating monomer chain). Carbon is the backbone of all of them because it forms four stable covalent bonds.

✓ Quick recall
Protein structure 4 levels: Primary (amino acid sequence) → Secondary (α-helix / β-pleated sheet, H-bonds in backbone) → Tertiary (3-D fold, R-group interactions) → Quaternary (multiple subunits). Denaturation = heat/pH unfolds the shape → loses function. Sequence dictates shape dictates function.

1.3Dehydration Synthesis vs. Hydrolysis

Two reactions run every macromolecule. They are mirror images — and water is the hinge.

⚠ The classic Do-Now question
Two glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) join to form maltose. Why is maltose C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ and not C₁₂H₂₄O₁₂?
Answer: dehydration synthesis removed one H₂O when forming the glycosidic bond. 12+12 C, but 24−2 = 22 H and 12−1 = 11 O. Every bond formed = one water out.
⚡ On the exam
Digestion is hydrolysis (enzymes + water break food polymers into absorbable monomers). Building body tissue/storage is dehydration synthesis. Both require enzymes.
UNIT 2

Cell Structure & Function

Organelles · Membrane · Transport

2.1Cell Theory & Two Kinds of Cells

Cell theory: (1) all living things are made of cells, (2) the cell is the basic unit of life, (3) all cells come from pre-existing cells.

Prokaryote

  • No nucleus — DNA loose in nucleoid
  • No membrane-bound organelles
  • Small, simple, bacteria/archaea
  • Has: ribosomes, cell wall, plasma membrane, cytoplasm
  • Better at: reproducing fast, surviving extremes

Eukaryote

  • True nucleus (membrane-bound DNA)
  • Membrane-bound organelles
  • Larger, complex, plant/animal/fungi/protist
  • Compartments = specialized reactions
  • Better at: specialization, large multicellular bodies
✓ Quick recall — structure ↔ function (heavily tested)
  • RBC has no nucleus (in mammals) → maximizes O₂-carrying volume; biconcave for surface area. Trap: frog RBCs DO have a nucleus.
  • Neuron — long axon + dendrites to transmit signals far & fast.
  • Muscle cell — packed with mitochondria & striated proteins for ATP-fueled contraction.
  • Root hair cell — long projection for water/mineral absorption; no chloroplasts (underground).

2.2The Eukaryotic Cell

An organelle is to a cell what an organ is to a body: a structure with a specialized job. Plant cells add three things animals lack: cell wall, chloroplasts, large central vacuole.

⚠ Classic traps
  • Cytoskeleton = microtubules + microfilaments + intermediate filaments. The one that anchors the nucleus in place is intermediate filaments.
  • Cytosol = the fluid; cytoplasm = fluid + everything in it; cytoskeleton = the protein scaffold. Don't mix these up.
  • Bound ribosomes make proteins destined to be secreted or membrane-embedded; free ribosomes make cytoplasmic proteins.

2.3The Plasma Membrane

The fluid mosaic model: a phospholipid bilayer studded with proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrate tags — fluid because the phospholipids drift sideways.

⚡ On the exam
Why can lipid-soluble (nonpolar, small) molecules cross but water-soluble (polar, charged, large) cannot? The hydrophobic tail core repels polar/charged things. So O₂, CO₂, steroids slip through; ions, glucose, water (slowly) need protein channels. Membrane protein jobs: transport, enzymes, receptors (signaling), cell recognition, attachment.

2.4Transport & Osmosis

Passive — no ATP, high → low

  • Simple diffusion — small nonpolar straight through
  • Facilitated diffusion — polar/large via a protein, still down the gradient (e.g. glucose, ions)
  • Osmosis — diffusion of water across a membrane

Active — needs ATP, low → high

  • Pumps — e.g. Na⁺/K⁺ pump, against the gradient
  • Endocytosis — cell engulfs material IN (vesicle forms)
  • Exocytosis — vesicle fuses, dumps material OUT

Molecules diffuse because of random motion + a concentration gradient; we track net movement (both directions happen, net is high→low) until equilibrium.

✓ Quick recall — say it out loud
"Hyper = more solute outside → water OUT → cell shrinks. Hypo = less solute outside → water IN → cell swells/lyses. Iso = equal → no net change." Water moves toward solute.
⚡ On the exam
Marine organisms live in a hypertonic ocean → constantly lose water (must drink & pump out salt). Freshwater organisms live in a hypotonic environment → constantly gain water (must pump it out, e.g. contractile vacuole). Osmoregulation is a "characteristic of life" theme.
⚠ Plant vs. animal in hypotonic
Hypotonic is dangerous for animal cells (lyse) but ideal for plant cells — the rigid cell wall lets them become firm/turgid instead of bursting. A wilted plant is in a hypertonic/dry situation (plasmolysis).
UNIT 3

Cellular Energetics

Enzymes · Photosynthesis · Respiration

3.1Enzymes & ATP

ATP is the cell's energy currency: breaking off the third phosphate (ATP → ADP + Pi) releases energy. Enzymes are protein catalysts that lower activation energy so reactions go faster — they are not used up and don't change ΔG.

  • Specificity: the substrate fits the enzyme's active site like a lock & key / induced fit. Names usually end in -ase.
  • Affected by: temperature, pH, substrate & enzyme concentration. Too hot or wrong pH → denatures (active site loses shape → stops working).
  • Inhibition: competitive (blocks active site) vs. noncompetitive/allosteric (binds elsewhere, changes shape). Feedback inhibition = the end product shuts off its own pathway.
⚡ On the exam
A graph of rate vs. temperature/pH peaks at the optimum then crashes (denaturation) — it does not keep rising. Rate vs. substrate concentration plateaus (all enzymes saturated). Know which graph is which.

3.2Photosynthesis deep — high yield

Plants convert light energy into chemical energy (sugar). Happens in the chloroplast, in two linked stages.

6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂

Stage 1 — Light Reactions (in the thylakoid membrane)

  1. Photosystem II absorbs light → energizes electrons. Water is split (photolysis): replaces lost e⁻, releases O₂ as byproduct, and dumps H⁺ into the lumen.
  2. Electrons fall down the electron transport chain; the cytochrome complex pumps H⁺ into the lumen → builds a gradient.
  3. Photosystem I re-energizes electrons → used to make NADPH.
  4. H⁺ flows back out through ATP synthase → chemiosmosis makes ATP (ADP + Pi → ATP).

Stage 2 — Calvin Cycle (in the stroma)

Uses the ATP + NADPH to fix CO₂ into sugar. Three phases — track it as a wheel that turns 3× to net one G3P:

⚡ On the exam — must know cold
  • O₂ comes from splitting WATER, not CO₂. (Super common MCQ.)
  • Light reactions: input light + H₂O → output ATP + NADPH + O₂. Calvin: input CO₂ + ATP + NADPH → output G3P (sugar).
  • Calvin cycle is "light-independent" but stops in the dark because it runs out of ATP/NADPH.
  • RuBisCO = the CO₂-fixing enzyme (most abundant protein on Earth).
— Safe to skip
C4 / CAM plant photorespiration pathways, exact wavelengths/absorption-spectrum nm values, and the names of every electron carrier (plastoquinone, ferredoxin, etc.). Know chlorophyll absorbs red/blue, reflects green and that's why leaves look green.

3.3Cellular Respiration

The reverse logic: break glucose to harvest ATP. Aerobic (with O₂) yields ~36–38 ATP per glucose.

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂ → 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + ATP
✓ Quick recall — the mirror
Photosynthesis and respiration are opposites: photosynthesis stores energy & builds glucose (in chloroplast); respiration releases energy & breaks glucose (in mitochondria). Both use an electron transport chain + chemiosmosis (ATP synthase) — the universal ATP trick.
⚡ On the exam
Without O₂ → fermentation. Glycolysis still makes 2 ATP, then regenerates NAD⁺ via: lactic acid (animals/muscle) or alcohol + CO₂ (yeast/plants). No O₂ = no ETC = tiny ATP yield. Know O₂ is the final electron acceptor in the ETC (forms water).
— Safe to skip
Every intermediate (citrate, isocitrate, α-ketoglutarate…), exact ATP-per-NADH stoichiometry debates, and the pyruvate-oxidation "link reaction" details. Know inputs/outputs and where each stage happens.
UNIT 4

Cell Cycle & Communication

Mitosis · Regulation · Cancer

4.1The Cell Cycle & Mitosis

Interphase (G1 → S → G2) is most of a cell's life: it grows, then copies its DNA in S phase, then preps to divide. Mitosis + cytokinesis splits one cell into two genetically identical daughters.

✓ Quick recall
Animal cytokinesis = cleavage furrow pinches in. Plant cytokinesis = cell plate forms a new wall (no pinching — rigid wall). Mitosis result: 2 cells, diploid, genetically identical to parent. Used for growth, repair, asexual reproduction.

4.2Regulation, Checkpoints & Cancer

The cycle is policed at checkpoints (G1, G2, M) that ask: is the DNA okay? is the cell big enough? are chromosomes attached? If not, the cycle halts for repair.

  • Proto-oncogenes → "go" signals. Mutate into oncogenes = gas pedal stuck down.
  • Tumor-suppressor genes (e.g. p53) → "stop/repair" signals. Mutate = brakes cut.
  • Cancer = unregulated cell division → ignores checkpoints & apoptosis → forms a tumor; metastasis = spread.
⚡ On the exam
Cancer needs multiple mutations in genes controlling the cycle (it's not one switch). p53 is the famous tumor suppressor / "guardian of the genome." Cell signaling (ligand → receptor → signal transduction → response) is how cells coordinate division — a growth factor binding a receptor is the trigger.
— Safe to skip
Cyclin/CDK molecular names and concentration curves, the full kinase cascade steps, and second-messenger chemistry (cAMP details). Know the concept: checkpoints + signals regulate division; losing that control = cancer.
UNIT 5

Heredity

Meiosis · Mendel · Chi-Square · Linkage

5.1Meiosis

Meiosis makes gametes (sex cells): one diploid (2n) cell → four haploid (n) cells that are genetically different. Two divisions, one DNA replication.

⚡ On the exam — phase ID (heavily tested)
  • DNA replication → Interphase (S phase), before meiosis even starts.
  • Crossing over & synapsis (homologs pair as a tetrad, swap segments at the chiasma) → Prophase I.
  • Independent assortment (homolog pairs line up & orient randomly) → Metaphase I.
  • Crossing over + independent assortment + random fertilization = genetic variation.
✓ Chromosome math
If diploid 2n = 28 → each gamete has 14; a zygote from two such gametes = back to 28. Mitosis keeps the number; meiosis halves it; fertilization restores it.

Mitosis

  • 1 division → 2 cells
  • Diploid, identical to parent
  • Growth, repair, asexual
  • No crossing over / no tetrads

Meiosis

  • 2 divisions → 4 cells
  • Haploid, all different
  • Makes gametes (sexual repro)
  • Crossing over + independent assortment

5.2Mendelian Genetics

  • Law of Segregation: the two alleles for a gene separate into different gametes (one per gamete).
  • Law of Independent Assortment: genes on different chromosomes are inherited independently.
  • Dominant masks recessive; genotype (alleles, e.g. Bb) vs phenotype (trait shown); homozygous vs heterozygous.
✓ Non-Mendelian patterns
Incomplete dominance = blend (red × white → pink). Codominance = both show (AB blood). Multiple alleles = >2 versions (ABO: IA, IB, i). Sex-linked = gene on X → males (XY) show recessive traits more (hemophilia, colorblindness). Polygenic = many genes (height, skin).

5.3Chi-Square — The Full Workflow do exactly this

Chi-square asks: "Is the difference between what I observed and what I expected just chance, or is something real going on?"

χ² = Σ (observed − expected)² / expected df = (# categories − 1)
▶ Worked example — dihybrid F₂, 9:3:3:1

A cross predicts a 9:3:3:1 ratio. You count 556 offspring: 315, 108, 101, 32.

Step 1 — State H₀ (null): "The offspring follow the expected 9:3:3:1 Mendelian ratio (the genes assort independently). Any difference is due to chance."
Step 2 — Expected = total × ratio fraction. 556 × 9/16 = 312.75; ×3/16 = 104.25; ×3/16 = 104.25; ×1/16 = 34.75.
Step 3 — Compute each (O−E)²/E:
(315−312.75)²/312.75 = 0.016 (108−104.25)²/104.25 = 0.135 (101−104.25)²/104.25 = 0.101 ( 32− 34.75)²/ 34.75 = 0.218 χ² = 0.016+0.135+0.101+0.218 = 0.47
Step 4 — df = 4 categories − 1 = 3. Critical value at p = 0.05, df = 3 → 7.81.
Step 5 — Decide & write the conclusion in these words:
Since χ² (0.47) < critical value (7.81), we fail to reject the null hypothesis. There is not statistical evidence that the offspring deviate from the expected Mendelian ratio — the difference is due to chance.
⚠ The decision rule — never get this backwards
  • χ² < critical value → FAIL TO REJECT H₀. Data fits the expected ratio. "No statistical evidence that it deviates."
  • χ² > critical value → REJECT H₀. Data does not fit. Something real is going on (e.g. genes are linked, not independently assorting).
  • p = 0.05 is the cutoff. df = (categories − 1), not sample size − 1.

5.4Linkage & Recombination Frequency

Linked genes sit close together on the same chromosome → inherited together → fewer recombinants than expected. Crossing over occasionally separates them; the more often = the farther apart.

RF = (# recombinant offspring) / (total offspring) × 100% RF% ≈ map distance in centimorgans (cM)
▶ Worked example — recombination frequency

A test cross gives four phenotypes. The two large classes are parental; the two small classes are recombinants:

parental: 420 + 400 = 820 recombinant: 90 + 90 = 180 total = 1000 RF = 180 / 1000 = 18% → genes ≈ 18 cM apart

Smaller RF (e.g. 90/1000 = 9% → 9 cM) = genes are closer together & more tightly linked. RF maxes out near 50% (then they behave as unlinked / independently assorting).

⚠ Classic traps
  • Recombinants are the SMALL groups, parentals are the large groups. Don't add the wrong pair.
  • RF can never exceed 50%. 50% = effectively unlinked.
  • This connects to chi-square: if a 1:1:1:1 testcross fails the chi-square (you reject H₀), that's your evidence the genes are linked — then report RF as the map distance.
UNIT 6

Gene Expression & Regulation

DNA · Transcription · Translation

6.1DNA Structure & Replication

DNA is a double helix of nucleotides (phosphate + deoxyribose sugar + nitrogen base). The two strands are antiparallel and held by base pairs.

⚡ On the exam
Chargaff: %A = %T and %G = %C. If a strand is given, write the complement (A↔T, G↔C). Semiconservative = each daughter DNA keeps one parent strand. DNA → RNA → protein is the central dogma.

6.2Transcription (DNA → mRNA)

In the nucleus, RNA polymerase reads the DNA template strand and builds a complementary mRNA. RNA differences: single strand, ribose sugar, and uracil (U) replaces thymine (T).

▶ Worked — DNA template → mRNA
DNA template (3'→5'): T A C G G A T T T A C T mRNA (5'→3'): A U G C C U A A A U G A (start) (stop)
Rule: A→U, T→A, C→G, G→C. T becomes U. AUG = start codon; UAA/UAG/UGA = stop.

6.3Translation (mRNA → protein)

At the ribosome, mRNA is read in 3-base codons. Each codon is matched by a tRNA carrying an anticodon and the matching amino acid. Amino acids link via peptide bonds → polypeptide → protein.

⚠ Mutations
Point/substitution: silent (same aa, redundant code), missense (different aa), nonsense (early stop — usually worst). Frameshift (insertion/deletion of non-multiples of 3) shifts every codon downstream — typically catastrophic. Use the codon chart: read mRNA, not DNA.
✓ Gene regulation (concept level)
Every cell has the same DNA but expresses different genes → that's why a muscle cell ≠ a neuron. Prokaryotes: operons (e.g. lac operon — turns on genes only when lactose is present). Eukaryotes: transcription factors, regulatory sequences. Differential gene expression = the answer to "how do cells with identical DNA differ?"
— Safe to skip
RNA processing minutiae (spliceosome mechanism, every snRNP), exact lac/trp operon repressor kinetics, and Okazaki-fragment/ligase/primase naming. Know the central dogma flow, U-for-T, codon reading, and mutation types.
UNIT 7

Evolution & Natural Selection

Selection · Hardy–Weinberg · Phylogeny

7.1Natural Selection

Darwin's logic: organisms overproduce → there is variation → some variants survive & reproduce better (differential fitness) → those traits become more common over generations.

  • Fitness = reproductive success, not strength. The trait must be heritable to matter.
  • Selection acts on phenotypes; evolution = change in allele frequencies in a population over time (individuals don't evolve, populations do).
  • Evidence: fossils, homologous structures (common ancestry), vestigial structures, embryology, biogeography, molecular/DNA similarity, observed cases (antibiotic resistance, Galápagos finches).
✓ Sources of variation & non-selective change
Variation comes from mutation (original source) + sexual recombination. Allele frequencies also change by genetic drift (random, strong in small populations — bottleneck & founder effects) and gene flow (migration). Selection is the only non-random mechanism.

7.2Hardy–Weinberg plug & chug

A null model: if a population is not evolving, allele frequencies stay constant. Deviation = evolution is happening.

p + q = 1 p = freq(dominant allele), q = freq(recessive allele) p² + 2pq + q² = 1 p² = homo-dom, 2pq = hetero, q² = homo-recessive
▶ Worked — find the carriers

9% of a population shows the recessive disease (aa). Find allele & genotype frequencies.

q² = 0.09 → q = √0.09 = 0.3
p = 1 − q = 1 − 0.3 = 0.7
Carriers 2pq = 2(0.7)(0.3) = 0.42 → 42% are heterozygous carriers
p² = 0.49 → 49% homozygous dominant. Check: 0.49 + 0.42 + 0.09 = 1 ✓
⚡ On the exam
Always start from q² (the recessive phenotype) because it's the only genotype you can see directly. 5 conditions for equilibrium (= NOT evolving): no mutation, no selection, no gene flow (migration), random mating, very large population. Break any one → allele frequencies change → evolution.

7.3Phylogeny, Classification & Speciation

  • Taxonomy: Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species ("Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Soup"). Three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya.
  • Speciation = formation of new species when gene flow stops & populations diverge. Allopatric = geographic barrier; sympatric = same area (e.g. polyploidy in plants). Species = can interbreed & produce fertile offspring.
  • Homologous structures = same origin, divergent (common ancestor). Analogous = different origin, same function (convergent evolution — don't use for the tree).
⚠ Classic trap
Reading trees: relatedness = most recent common ancestor (shared node), NOT how close the tip labels are written or "how many branches." Analogous structures (bird vs. insect wing) show convergent evolution and must not be used to infer ancestry.
— Safe to skip
Cladogram parsimony calculation steps, molecular-clock math, every reproductive-isolation subtype name, and full kingdom characteristics tables. Know homologous vs analogous, allopatric vs sympatric, and how to read a tree.
UNIT 8

Ecology

Populations · Energy Flow · Cycles

8.1Population & Community Ecology

  • Density-dependent limits (kick in when crowded): competition, predation, disease, food. Density-independent: weather, natural disasters, fire.
  • Symbiosis: mutualism (++), commensalism (+0), parasitism (+−), predation (+−), competition (−−).
  • Niche = an organism's role. Competitive exclusion: two species can't occupy the identical niche indefinitely. Keystone species = disproportionately large effect on the community.
  • Succession: primary (from bare rock, starts with pioneer species/lichen) vs secondary (after disturbance, soil remains — faster).

8.2Energy Flow & Trophic Levels

Energy enters as sunlight, is captured by producers, and flows one way up the chain — only ~10% transfers between trophic levels (the rest is lost as heat / used in metabolism).

⚡ On the exam
Energy flow is one-way and lost (~90%) at each level; it is not recycled. Matter (C, N, H₂O) IS recycled via biogeochemical cycles. Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) return nutrients to the soil — without them, cycles stop. Producers = base of every pyramid.

8.3Biogeochemical Cycles

✓ Quick recall
Water cycle: evaporation/transpiration ↑, condensation → precipitation ↓, runoff & groundwater. The big idea across all cycles: matter cycles, energy flows. Human impact (fossil fuels → CO₂, fertilizer → N runoff → eutrophication) is fair game.
— Safe to skip
Every named bacterium genus, exact phosphorus-cycle reservoirs, and detailed nutrient-flux numbers. Know each cycle's main reservoir, how organisms move it, and that decomposers + bacteria are essential.
